## Number Swamp (7 remaining)
### mental-maths — Splatrick the Swift — weapon **The Tens-Friend Glove**: "Jump to the nearest TEN first, then jump the rest."
Teach: number bonds to 10/20; add/sub 2-digit mentally via tens-jumps (47+8 = 47+3+5); tables to 10×10 as skip-count anchors; division facts as reverse tables. T1: single-step bonds, tables (mcq/num); T2: two-digit ± two-digit mental, missing-number facts (7×☐=56); T3 num: chains (23+38−9), "how many 8s in 72". Traps: forgetting the carried ten; 7×8 confusion cluster (54/63/48 distractors); subtraction direction.
### written-methods — Borrowin' Barry — weapon **The Column Cannon**: "Line up the columns by throne, work right to left, and carry like a hero."
Teach: column add/sub with carrying/borrowing (show worked columns via `table` diagram); short multiplication (2-3 digit × 1 digit); short division with remainders; ÷ decimals by whole (money link); remainders IN CONTEXT: round UP for containers, DOWN for full teams/complete items. T1: 2-3 digit column ±; T2: ×/÷ with carrying, remainder as r-form; T3 num: context remainders ("197 pupils, 6 per bench → benches?"), decimal ÷ whole (7.5 ÷ 3). Traps: misaligned columns (place-value slip distractor), remainder direction (rounded wrong way), dropped carry.
### fractions — Halfbottom the Divided — weapon **The Fair-Share Blade**: "The bottom number says how many equal pieces. The top says how many you take."
Teach: fraction = equal parts of a whole (polygrid diagrams incl. IRREGULAR shaded grids — count carefully, shaded cells not adjacent); equivalence via times-tables on top and bottom (½=2/4=5/10); fraction of an amount (divide by bottom, × top); simple comparison. T1: shaded-grid naming (polygrid), equivalents; T2: fraction of amount (¾ of 24), compare/order simple; T3 num: harder of-amounts (⅗ of 45), equivalent missing number (2/3 = ☐/12). Traps: counting shaded vs unshaded; top/bottom swap; non-equal parts trick (grid with unequal regions is NEVER used as correct — teach why); fraction-of-remainder flagged as FDP-tier cross-trap.
### fdp — Percy Percent — weapon **The Disguise Detector**: "½, 0.5 and 50% are the SAME creature in three disguises. To find a fraction OF an amount: divide by the bottom, times by the top."
Teach: the equivalence triangle (½/0.5/50%, ¼/0.25/25%, ¾, ⅒, ⅕; tenths↔0.x↔x0%); percentage of amount via 10% anchor; **fraction-of-the-REMAINDER trap gets its own lesson card** (papers' favourite). T1: match disguises (mcq); T2: 25% of 60; 0.7 = ?%; order mixed FDP set; T3 num: 15% of 80 (10%+5%), remainder problems ("⅓ eaten, then half the REST…"). Traps: 0.5 = 5% slip; percent-of vs percent-off; of-original vs of-remainder.
### sequences — The Sequel — weapon **The Gap-o-Meter**: "Find the jump between neighbours FIRST. The jump reveals everything."
Teach: constant-step sequences both directions incl. crossing zero into negatives; doubling/halving; shape/growing patterns (polygrid growing squares); predicting far terms by pattern-not-listing ("the 30th"). T1: next term, missing middle term; T2: descending past zero, doubling, rule-in-words; T3 num: 30th-term via structure (n×step+start taught informally as "jumps from the start"), growing-pattern counts. Traps: sign slip at zero-crossing; off-by-one on far terms (29 jumps not 30); halving odd numbers (excluded — keep even).
### special-numbers — Prime Slime — weapon **The Prime Probe**: "A prime has exactly TWO factors: 1 and itself. 1 is not prime. 2 is the only even one."
Teach: factors & multiples first (arrays); primes to 30; squares to 12²; cubes of 2,3,4,5,10; triangular numbers via stacking (polygrid); indices notation (3² means 3×3, NOT 3×2). T1: is-it-prime, next square; T2: which is NOT a multiple/factor (NOT-question drilled here), square vs cube values; T3 num: 7²−?, list-then-compute ("sum of primes between 10 and 20"), triangular next. Traps: 1-is-prime; 3²=6 slip; 27 "looks prime"; factor/multiple word swap.
### machines-mystery — Backwards Bertha — weapon **The Reverse Lever**: "To find what went IN, run the machine BACKWARDS: undo each step with its opposite."
Teach: function machines forward (one/two step); REVERSE machines (the exam favourite); letter-for-number as "a machine wearing a disguise" (6+a=24 → a = 24−6); two-facts combos (a+12=27 AND b÷3=9 → a×b style, papers do this). T1: forward one-step; T2: reverse one-step, simple letter values; T3 num: reverse two-step, combined two-letter problems. Traps: forgetting to reverse the ORDER of a two-step machine; applying not-inverted op; letter treated as digit.

## Measure Marsh (7)
### metric-units — Centi-Peed — weapon **The Kilo-Centi-Milli Ladder**: "kilo = ×1000, centi = ÷100, milli = ÷1000. Slide the digits, never the point." (cross-reference the Slide-o-Matic lesson explicitly)
Teach: metre/gram/litre families; the ladder; conversions both ways (175 cm = 1.75 m); choosing sensible units (kg for food per spec); cm↔mm inside word problems. T1: single conversions ladder-adjacent (m↔cm, kg↔g); T2: mixed incl. mm, l↔ml, compare quantities in different units; T3 num: word-problem conversions ("ribbon 2.4 m, cut 65 cm…"— answer in stated unit, unit chip shown). Traps: slid wrong direction (×100 vs ÷100), cm↔mm ×10 not ×100, mixed-unit comparison without converting.
### clocks-time — The Minute Muncher — weapon **The Two-Face Watch**: "Past or to? Minute hand left half = TO, right half = PAST. For 24-hour: afternoon = add 12."
Teach: analogue reading (clock diagram) to 5 min and to the minute; a.m./p.m.; 12↔24-hour both ways; simple durations within an hour. T1: read clock diagram (mcq times); T2: 24-hr conversions (7.40 p.m. → 19:40), quarter-to/past; T3 num: duration to next event, write 24-hr time. Traps: hour hand between numbers (reading the NEXT hour early); 12:xx a.m./p.m. edge kept OUT of T1-2, one gentle T3 template only (midnight crossing lives in timetables); adding 12 to a.m. times.
### timetables — Bus-Conductor Bogface — weapon **The Finger-Track**: "One finger on the row, one on the column — read where they meet. For journey time, count UP to the next hour, then on."
Teach: reading a timetable grid (table diagram, 3 stops × 4 buses); journey duration via count-up; which bus to catch to arrive by X; elapsed time crossing midnight (papers do this at T3); calendars: day-of-week counting across a month boundary (table diagram calendar). T1: read-off cell; T2: journey length, best-bus; T3 num: crossing-midnight duration, calendar "what date is 12 days after Oct 24?". Traps: reading wrong column; subtracting clock times like normal numbers (8:20−7:45 ≠ 75); forgetting months have different lengths (stick to given calendar).
### temperature — Freezer Geezer — weapon **The Zero Bridge**: "Crossing zero? Count to zero first, then keep going. Two small jumps beat one big slip."
Teach: negative numbers on a vertical numline/thermometer diagram; difference between temperatures incl. both negative; rise/fall wording. T1: read thermometer, warmer/colder compare; T2: difference across zero (−3 to 8); T3 num: fall of 9 from 4; "difference between −8 and −2". Traps: counting the zero twice; −8 "bigger" than −2; direction of change sign.
### perimeter — The Perimeter Prowler — weapon **The Fence-Walker's Boots**: "Walk EVERY side and add as you go. Hidden sides still count — find them before you walk."
Teach: perimeter = total boundary walk (shape diagrams); rectangles via 2×(l+w); compound/L-shapes: DERIVE the hidden sides from given ones (the papers' hexagon-flower/hidden-edge trap gets a full card); regular polygons via ×sides. T1: rectangle/square perimeter, all sides labelled; T2: rectangle from l+w only, regular polygon; T3 num: L-shape with two sides to derive, joined-shapes shared-edge exclusion. Traps: adding only labelled sides; counting shared internal edges; area/perimeter confusion (classic distractor = the area value).
### area-volume — Cubby McSquareface — weapon **The Tile-and-Stack Kit**: "Area = tiles that cover it (length × width). Volume = stacked layers (× height too)."
Teach: area by counting squares (polygrid incl. part-shaded → whole-squares-only per KS2), rectangle area l×w with cm²; volume by counting cubes then l×w×h with cm³ (cuboid diagram); reverse: side from area. T1: count squares/cubes; T2: calculate area/volume from labelled diagrams, units correct; T3 num: reverse ("area 36 cm², one side 9 → other?"), decimal dimension (2.5 × 4), compound rectangle areas by splitting. Traps: perimeter instead of area; forgetting ² / ³ (unit given in chip — but mcq unit distractors used); adding not multiplying.
### scale-maps — The Shrinkler — weapon **The Shrink-Ray Reverser**: "Read the scale like a recipe: 1 cm means X in real life. Measure, then multiply."
Teach: simple scale statements (1 cm = 2 m); reading given measurements off a simple scaledrawing diagram (never asks child to physically measure — lengths are LABELLED per spec); real distance = drawing × scale; reverse gently at T3. T1: 1cm=5m, drawing 4 cm → real; T2: mixed scales, compare two routes; T3 num: reverse (real 24 m, scale 1cm=4m → drawing), scale with m→km ladder step. Traps: dividing instead of multiplying; unit of the ANSWER; adding scale instead of multiplying.

## Shape Caves (6)
### shapes-2d — Polly Gone — weapon **The Shape Sorter**: "Count the sides, check the angles, look for equal marks — the shape names itself."
Teach: triangle family (scalene/isosceles/equilateral/right-angled); quadrilateral family tree (square/rectangle/rhombus/kite/parallelogram/trapezium — property table); regular vs irregular; polygon names to octagon; circle parts light (radius/diameter). T1: name from shape diagram; T2: property→shape ("4 equal sides, no right angles"), true/false properties, NOT-questions; T3: selecttwo ("which TWO are always true of a rhombus"), odd-one-out with reason. Traps: square IS a rectangle (teach the family honestly, test gently); rhombus/kite confusion; regular = "looks normal" misconception.
### shapes-3d — Sir Facelot — weapon **The Face-Edge-Corner Counter**: "Faces are flat, edges are where faces meet, vertices are the corners. Count in that order."
Teach: cube/cuboid/cone/cylinder/sphere/triangular prism/square-based pyramid; faces-edges-vertices table (memorise cube 6-12-8); prisms = same slice all through; cube nets (net diagrams, which of these folds into a cube — the 11 nets, papers love this). T1: name the solid (diagram); T2: F/E/V counts, prism-or-not; T3: net validity (mcq of net diagrams), "which solid has 5 faces" style. Traps: cone/cylinder edge-vertex miscounts (curved surfaces); net corner-overlap classics (the T vs the +).
### angles-lines — Obtusius — weapon **The Angle Gauge**: "Sharper than a corner = acute. Past a corner = obtuse. Past a straight line = reflex. A triangle's angles always total 180°."
Teach: angle types incl. right-angle square mark; vertical/horizontal/perpendicular/parallel language; angles on a straight line = 180°; INTERNAL angles: triangle 180° (isosceles pairs!), quadrilateral 360°. Never measuring/drawing (spec) — all angle diagrams say "not to scale". T1: classify from angle diagram; line-language match; T2: missing angle on a line, in a triangle; T3 num: isosceles find-the-pair, quadrilateral missing fourth, two-step (angle on line THEN in triangle). Traps: treating diagram as to-scale; isosceles equal-pair choice; 360 vs 180 swap.
### turns-compass — Wrong-Way Wanda — weapon **The Compass Cap**: "N-E-S-W clockwise ('Naughty Elephants Squirt Water'). A quarter turn = one letter along."
Teach: 8 compass points; ¼/½/¾/full turns, clockwise/anti-; facing X, turn Y → now facing?; REVERSE turns (end given, find start — papers trap). T1: quarter-turns from N/E/S/W; T2: ¾ turns, anti-clockwise, 8-point turns (NE etc.); T3: reverse turns, multi-instruction chains. Traps: anti-clockwise direction slip; ¾ = 3 letters not 1; reverse = must invert direction.
### coordinates — Gridlock — weapon **The Along-Then-Up Ladder**: "ALONG the hall first, THEN up the stairs. (x, y) — always in that order."
Teach: first-quadrant plotting/reading (coordgrid diagram); shapes on grids; find the MISSING VERTEX of a rectangle/parallelogram (papers favourite); simple midpoints informally at T3. T1: read a point; T2: which point is at (3,5), plot-the-shape reads; T3 num: missing vertex ("write the coordinates" — accept "(4,7)" and "4,7"), point after a described move. Traps: (x,y) swap (the eternal one — every template carries a swapped distractor); counting lines vs squares.
### symmetry — Reflecto — weapon **The Fold Test**: "Imagine folding on the line — every point must land exactly on its twin."
Teach: line symmetry in shapes (how many lines: square 4, rectangle 2 NOT 4, equilateral 3, circle ∞ mentioned); diagonal mirror lines; completing a symmetric pattern (symmetry-grid diagram); reflected position of a marked cell. T1: has-it-got-symmetry, count lines in simple shapes; T2: rectangle-4-lines trap, diagonal reflections; T3: complete-the-reflection cell coordinates (polygrid), letters of the alphabet symmetry. Traps: rectangle diagonal "line of symmetry" (it is NOT); reflecting translation-style (sliding not flipping).

Teach: money notation (£3.40 not £3.4 — calculator display card: "£3.4 on a calculator MEANS £3.40"); add/subtract money columns; multiply single item price; unit-price-then-reapply (papers trap); best value informally; estimation with money (link to Catapult Hill). ### change-coins — The Changeling — weapon **The Change-Counter's Count-Up**: "Don't subtract — COUNT UP from the price to what you paid."

T1: change from £1/£5 round prices; T2: change from £10 odd prices, fewest coins for ≤£1; T3 num: fewest coins count for £3.67-style, change split across two items. Traps: subtract-digit-wise slip (change from £5 for £3.65 → £2.45 distractor); counting coins not value; forgetting a coin denomination doesn't exist (no 25p!).

## Chance Cliffs (1)
### probability — Maybe-Marvin — weapon **The Chance Compass**: "Impossible–unlikely–evens–likely–certain. For dice: wanted outcomes out of six."
Teach: the likelihood line (certain/likely/evens/unlikely/impossible/fair as vocabulary); ordering events by likelihood; fifty-fifty = evens; dice & spinner chances as "x out of 6" (spinner diagram); comparing two probability STATEMENTS (papers style). T1: place event on likelihood line; is this fair?; T2: dice "chance of even number", spinner sector chance; T3: order 3-4 events, which statement is true (selecttwo occasionally), "3 out of 6 is the same as…" bridge to ½. Traps: recency thinking ("6 is due!") called out in lesson; confusing unlikely with impossible; fair ≠ "nice".

## The Data Dump (4)
### graphs-charts — Barry Chart — weapon **The Scale-First Spyglass**: "Read the SCALE before the bars — one square is not always one."
Teach: bar chart reading (barchart diagram, yStep 2/5/10!), bar-line and simple line graphs (linegraph, axis from zero per spec), pictograms with half-symbols, block graphs; comparisons and totals FROM charts; Venn diagrams (venn — counts in regions incl. "neither"). T1: read a bar; tallest/shortest; T2: difference between bars, pictogram half-symbol, venn region count; T3 num: total across all bars, two-step ("how many more A than B and C together"), venn "in A but not B". Traps: scale-interval misread (THE big one — every template's key distractor); pictogram half-symbol; venn overlap double-count.
### tables-tally — Tally Wally — weapon **The Gate Counter**: "Tally gates come in fives: four sticks and a gate. Count gates, then strays."
Teach: tally marks → frequency; frequency tables; dense many-column tables read systematically (row finger, column finger — cross-ref Finger-Track); completing a table from tallies. T1: read a tally count; single cell lookup; T2: table difference/total, missing cell from row total; T3 num: multi-condition lookup ("how many Year 6 girls chose swimming"), grand totals. Traps: gate = 5 not 4; row/column swap; subtotal vs grand total.
### pie-charts — Pie-Face — weapon **The Slice Splitter**: "The whole pie = everything = 360°. Half the pie = half of everything."
Teach: pie = whole; half/quarter slices as fractions OF THE TOTAL (pie diagram); simple interpretation ("which was most popular"); comparing slices; frequency-table → pie ANGLE (each item = 360÷total degrees — papers T3). T1: biggest/smallest slice; T2: "half of 40 people chose…", quarter-slice values; T3 num: angle for one item ("18 of 36 → 180°"), value from a labelled angle. Traps: slice size vs count when totals differ (two pies never compared without totals — teach the warning); 360 vs 100 confusion.
### mean-range — The Meanie — weapon **The Sharing-Out Scoop**: "Mean = share the total out equally (add all, divide by how many). Range = biggest minus smallest."
Teach: mean as fair-sharing (concrete: level the towers, polygrid); the two-step ritual (ADD then DIVIDE); range as spread; find mean/range of 3-5 small numbers; missing-value-given-mean gently at T3. T1: range of a set; mean of easy triple; T2: mean of 4-5 values, compare two sets' ranges; T3 num: mean with a decimal answer avoided (sets constructed to divide cleanly), missing value ("mean of 4 numbers is 7; three are 5, 6, 9 — the fourth?"). Traps: forgetting the divide (distractor = the total); dividing by wrong count; range = biggest number alone.
